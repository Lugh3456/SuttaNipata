第四品·第十五经 · 执杖经

经集 · Sutta Nipata

← 第四品·第十四经 目录 Contents 第四品·第十六经 →

以下经文据汉译《义足经》（T0198，支谦译，三国·吴）

执杖者互相恐怖，看！众生在世间—— 我将说厌离心，如我所见所知。 看！诸人在世间，如鱼在少水中—— 互相竞争苦恼，见此生出离心。 世间全皆不实，四方皆颠覆—— 欲得住处所，我观不见之。 乃至究竟处，皆见诤论有—— 见此已厌离，求不动摇法。 见人执诸见，如水中鱼相争—— 我说此寂静，不著于任何—— 无欲无烟雾，无渴无爱著—— 渡越轮回苦，此为牟尼所行。 弃诸欲爱著，渡越轮回泥—— 于内无执著，于外无依止—— 不思过去事，不贪现在乐—— 不欲将来有，善护诸根门—— 心中无惊惶，无慢无染垢—— 言语真实善，不乱不嗔恚—— 于施与受中，皆无喜怒相—— 此名为牟尼，到彼岸超越。

逐句解释

执杖者互相恐怖，看！众生在世间——我将说厌离心，如我所见所知。看！诸人在世间，如鱼在少水中——互相竞争苦恼，见此生出离心。

执杖经（Attadaṇḍa Sutta，attadaṇḍa = 执杖，字面意为「自执武器」）是八偈品中最强烈地呈现暴力与世间苦的经典。「执杖者互相恐怖」——人们手持武器，互相恐吓（这描绘的是暴力的世间图景）。「如鱼在少水中——互相竞争苦恼」：鱼在水干涸时互相竞争有限的空间，这是世间资源竞争与相互伤害的经典比喻。见到这个景象，「我将说厌离心」——经文的情感基调是厌离（nibbidā）：对暴力世间的深刻厌倦，驱动出离。

Those who take up weapons are terrified of each other — look! Beings in the world. I will speak of the impulse to turn away, as I have seen and known it. Look! People in the world are like fish in little water — suffering from mutual competition; seeing this, the impulse to leave arises.

The Attadaṇḍa Sutta opens with a panoramic view of human violence and competition. 'Taking up weapons' (attadaṇḍa) — the mutual arming of beings who fear each other — is the trigger for the teaching. The fish-in-drying-water simile captures the desperation of competitive existence: when resources become scarce, beings turn on each other. The impulse to turn away (nibbidā — revulsion or disenchantment, a key term in the liberating sequence) arises directly from seeing clearly. This is the Aṭṭhakavagga's most socially engaged opening — starting from the collective human condition of violent competition rather than individual philosophical analysis.

世间全皆不实，四方皆颠覆——欲得住处所，我观不见之。乃至究竟处，皆见诤论有——见此已厌离，求不动摇法。

「世间全皆不实，四方皆颠覆」——世间没有任何稳固可靠的地方（aniketaṃ，无家可居）。「欲得住处所，我观不见之」——寻找一个安全的住所（āvāsa，居所），在这个颠覆的世间中找不到。「乃至究竟处，皆见诤论有」：连在精神修行的领域，也充满了见争（vivāda）。「见此已厌离，求不动摇法」：见到一切处都不稳固、都有争论，因此转向寻求「不动摇之法」（āneñja，不动，即涅槃的一个名称）。

The whole world is without substance, all quarters are overturned — wanting a place to settle, I look but do not find one. Even at the furthest extent, I see only contentions — having seen this and felt revulsion, I seek the unshakeable teaching.

The search for a 'place to settle' (āvāsa — a resting-place, a home) is the Attadaṇḍa's unique contribution to the Aṭṭhakavagga's imagery of homelessness. The world offers no stable dwelling — not in physical space, not in social arrangements, not even in the domain of spiritual teachings (where contentions proliferate). The 'unshakeable' (āneñja) is one of the Pali Canon's epithets for Nibbāna — the place that does not move because nothing in it is conditioned and therefore nothing in it can be undermined by change.

不思过去事，不贪现在乐——不欲将来有，善护诸根门——心中无惊惶，无慢无染垢——言语真实善，不乱不嗔恚——于施与受中，皆无喜怒相——此名为牟尼，到彼岸超越。

执杖经的后半以牟尼的生活方式作具体描述：「不思过去事」——不执取过去；「不贪现在乐」——不执取现在；「不欲将来有」——不渴望未来的存在；「善护诸根门」——守护感官入口（防止不善的感知触发执取）；「心中无惊惶，无慢无染垢」——内心平稳，无傲慢无污染；「言语真实善，不乱不嗔恚」——正语；「于施与受中，皆无喜怒相」——给予或接受时都无喜无怒，彻底的平等心。这是八偈品中对牟尼日常生活状态最全面的描述。

Not thinking of the past, not clinging to present pleasures — not wanting future existence, guarding the sense faculties well — no agitation in the mind, no conceit, no defilement — speech that is true and good, not confused, not angry — in both giving and receiving, showing no sign of joy or anger — this is called the sage, crossed to the far shore, beyond.

The Attadaṇḍa's closing portrait of the sage is the Aṭṭhakavagga's most behaviorally concrete: non-attachment to past, present, and future; sense-restraint; equanimity of mind; right speech; and equanimity in all transactions. 'No sign of joy or anger' in giving and receiving is a particularly fine observation: even the social dynamics of generosity and gratitude are sites of potential attachment and aversion for the unguarded mind. The sage who traverses these moments without generating elation or irritation has achieved a quality of equanimity that is not coldness but the complete settling of conditioned reactivity.

总结 · Summary

执杖经是八偈品最具社会视角的经典，从人类集体暴力与竞争（执杖者互相恐怖，如鱼在少水）出发，生起厌离（nibbidā）。世间没有任何稳固的「住处」——物质的、社会的、精神的一切处，都是不实的、有争论的。由此转向寻求「不动摇之法」（涅槃）。经文后半以最全面的方式描述牟尼的日常状态：不著过去、现在、未来；守护根门；心无惊惶、慢、染垢；正语；施受之际皆无喜怒。这是八偈品中对牟尼实际生活方式最详细的具体描述，将哲学上的「无执取」落实为可观察的行为特征与心理状态。

The Attadaṇḍa Sutta is the Aṭṭhakavagga's most socially grounded text, beginning from the collective human condition — mutual armed terror, fish competing in drying water — as the ground for the impulse toward liberation. The world offers no stable resting-place in any domain; even spiritual practice is plagued by contention. The turn toward the unshakeable (āneñja — Nibbāna's epithet as the place nothing can destabilise) is motivated by this total homelessness. The closing portrait of the sage's daily life is the most concrete in the Aṭṭhakavagga: non-attachment to past, present, and future; sense-restraint; equanimity in mind and speech; and the absence of elation or irritation in all giving and receiving. The philosophical non-clinging has been translated into visible human behaviour.
