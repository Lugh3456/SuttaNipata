放下对一切生命的暴力，不伤害其中任何一个；不渴求有子，何况有伴，独行如犀角。 与伴同处，生起爱恋；爱恋之后，生起苦痛。见爱恋所生之过患，独行如犀角。 怜悯朋友，心挂情谊，便错失了自己的目标；见此缚缠之过患，独行如犀角。 如林中麋鹿，不受系缚，随意去到觅食之处——智者见自由之利，独行如犀角。 同伴之中，有人相邀，有人应邀；在无邀请的独处中，不依此也不依彼，独行如犀角。 与伴同处，有嬉戏玩乐，有对子女深深的爱；不愿离开爱子——独行如犀角。 四方无有怨恨，随所欲而知足，能忍受诸苦，无所畏惧——独行如犀角。 有些出家者难以相处，也有些在家者难以相处；对他人的子女少有关怀——独行如犀角。 舍弃在家众之标志，如柯维罗树脱去其叶，出家英勇者斩断在家之缚——独行如犀角。 若遇智慧的同行者，品行善良、坚定不移，克服一切危难，欢喜与他同行。 若不遇智慧的同行者，品行善良、坚定不移，如被击败的国王舍弃其国——独行如犀角。 当称赞结伴之乐，此乃真实；但若遇不到胜己或等己之友——独行如犀角。 见到精心打造、光彩夺目的黄金手镯，两只在一臂上叮当作响——独行如犀角。 如此，与他人同处，便会有话语争论；预见此过患——独行如犀角。 欲乐的享受，种种多彩，令人愉悦，以种种方式扰乱心——见欲乐之过患——独行如犀角。 此乃我的灾祸，痈疮、苦痛、疾病、箭矢——见欲乐之恐惧——独行如犀角。 寒与热、饥与渴、风与日的炙烤，虻与蚊虫——忍受一切——独行如犀角。 如大象舍弃象群，在林中独行游荡，所行随意——独行如犀角。 对于那爱好聚会、欢乐相聚之人，解脱的时候未曾到来；听取太阳亲族之语——独行如犀角。 已超越见解、已达到确定、已获得道的人，我不用这种方式引导他——独行如犀角。 不贪、不欺、不渴爱，去除了虚伪，在世间洗尽了烦恼与愚痴——独行如犀角。 避开恶知识，那些不以福祉为目标的人；自身不依附贪婪爱好者——独行如犀角。 结交博学之士，法义精通者，了知义理者，克服疑惑，独行如犀角。 世间的游戏玩乐，对我无所滋养，不含丝毫娱乐；不顾世间庄严——独行如犀角。 放下对子女的渴爱，更何况对同伴？如白莲花不为水所染——独行如犀角。 在各方面克服贪欲，心无恶意，超越一切苦乐——独行如犀角。 为了实现真正的至善，以利益为目标，克服五种障碍，不走弯路——独行如犀角。 放下悦意与不悦，离开一切依止，征服一切依附——独行如犀角。 为了最高的利益，已舍弃欲乐，不障碍任何人，不去伤害众生——独行如犀角。 如火燃烧时不回头，斩断贪欲与嗔恨——独行如犀角。 在愤怒中离开，在嗔恚中离开，在欺骗中离开，在愚痴中离开——独行如犀角。 抛开善恶，将执取干净去除，清净无染，无有烦恼——独行如犀角。 以利益为方向，清净、精进、思维深邃，在诸法中已确立——独行如犀角。 如狮子不被声音所惊，如风不被网所捕，如莲花不被水所染——独行如犀角。 如狮子以牙齿征服强大的兽类，统率群兽而游行，在偏远处栖息——独行如犀角。 时时修习慈、悲、喜、舍与解脱，不被世界所阻碍——独行如犀角。 放弃贪与嗔，舍弃痴，去除诸结，不畏死——独行如犀角。 有人为利益而结交，如今友谊因利益而存在；无求而交之友，今日难觅——独行如犀角。 在世间察知自身之利，不谋害他人，独行如犀角。 如林中大象舍弃象群，不受系缚——独行如犀角。

逐句解释

放下对一切生命的暴力，不伤害其中任何一个；不渴求有子，何况有伴，独行如犀角。

不对任何生命施以暴力，连子女都不渴求，更不要说同伴——在这样的无执中，独自前行，如犀牛角般专一。

Laying aside violence toward all beings, harming not even one of them — desiring no child, let alone a companion — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

The sutta opens with its most foundational instruction: non-violence extended to all beings, then non-clinging extended from children to companions. The rhinoceros horn — single, unforked, pointing in one direction — is the sutta's central image of undivided purpose. The horn has no companions; it simply is what it is, moving through the world without entanglement.

与伴同处，生起爱恋；爱恋之后，生起苦痛。见爱恋所生之过患，独行如犀角。

与伴相处，必生爱恋；爱恋生起，苦痛随之而来。看清爱恋所生的种种过患，独行如犀角。

With companions, affection arises; in the wake of affection, this suffering comes. Seeing the danger born from affection — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

The chain is stated plainly: companionship → affection → suffering. This is not a counsel of coldness but of clear seeing. Affection for persons is beautiful and natural; the sutta asks us to see the suffering that is its inseparable companion — the grief of separation, the fear of loss, the entanglement that diverts energy from liberation.

怜悯朋友，心挂情谊，便错失了自己的目标；见此缚缠之过患，独行如犀角。

对朋友心生怜悯、情谊系挂，便会错失自己的修行目标。看清这种缠缚的过患，独行如犀角。

One who is compassionate toward friends, one whose mind is bound up with others, loses sight of one's own goal. Seeing this danger in intimacy — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

Even compassion — one of the highest qualities — can become a form of bondage when it ties the mind to others' conditions. The practitioner who cannot sit quietly because a friend is suffering, who cannot concentrate because a loved one is ill, finds their own practice perpetually deferred. The sutta is not asking for indifference but for a kind of compassion that does not eclipse one's own path.

如林中麋鹿，不受系缚，随意去到觅食之处——智者见自由之利，独行如犀角。

如同林中的鹿，不受任何系缚，自由地去往觅食之所——智者见到自由的利益，独行如犀角。

Like a deer in the forest that is not tethered and goes wherever it wishes for pasture — the wise one, seeing freedom's benefit, wanders alone like a rhinoceros horn.

The deer image appears here for the first time (it recurs later in the sutta) and offers the positive side of what has been described by negation. The untethered deer is not lonely — it is free. It grazes where the grass is good. The wise practitioner similarly follows the nourishment of the Dhamma wherever it leads, unimpeded by fixed social obligations.

同伴之中，有人相邀，有人应邀；在无邀请的独处中，不依此也不依彼，独行如犀角。

在有同伴的地方，总有邀请与被邀请；在无邀无应的独处中，不依附任何一方——独行如犀角。

Among companions there are invitations to give and invitations to accept; in solitude without invitation there is no such reliance on this or on that — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

Social life is built on reciprocity — giving and receiving, inviting and being invited. Each exchange creates obligation and dependency. The solitary practitioner steps outside this web: no one to invite, no one to accept from, no social debt accumulating. This is not antisocial; it is a different kind of social relationship with the world — open, non-obligatory, undivided.

与伴同处，有嬉戏玩乐，有对子女深深的爱；不愿离开爱子——独行如犀角。

与伴相处，有嬉戏娱乐，有对子女深切的爱恋；不忍离开所爱的子女——独行如犀角。

With companions there is play and delight, and great love for children — the unwillingness to depart from beloved offspring — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

The sutta acknowledges what it is asking the practitioner to relinquish: not hardship but warmth. Play, delight, parental love — these are among life's greatest joys. The teaching does not pretend otherwise. It simply asks whether, given these joys and their inherent griefs, one is willing to choose the path of radical non-attachment. For many people the answer is no — and the sutta is addressed to those for whom it is yes.

四方无有怨恨，随所欲而知足，能忍受诸苦，无所畏惧——独行如犀角。

对四方众生无有怨恨，随处知足，能忍受种种苦难，无所畏惧——独行如犀角。

With no enmity in any direction, content with whatever comes, able to endure hardships, unfrightened — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

Four qualities for the solitary wanderer: no enemies (goodwill toward all), contentment (freedom from craving for better conditions), endurance (ability to bear what arises), and fearlessness (no terror of the unknown). Together they describe someone who needs nothing from the world and fears nothing from it — the natural condition of one who has genuinely released attachment.

有些出家者难以相处，也有些在家者难以相处；对他人的子女少有关怀——独行如犀角。

无论是出家众还是在家众，都有难以相处之人；对他人子女的关怀有限——独行如犀角。

Some renunciants are hard to live with; some householders too are difficult; caring little for the children of others — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

A frank acknowledgement that community life, even monastic life, is not frictionless. People are difficult. Communal arrangements generate their own conflicts and dissatisfactions. The verse does not recommend misanthropy but recognises that the frictions of communal living are real — and for the solitary practitioner, they are simply absent.

舍弃在家众之标志，如柯维罗树脱去其叶，出家英勇者斩断在家之缚——独行如犀角。

放弃在家生活的种种标志，如同柯维罗树脱尽叶子，英勇出家者斩断了世俗的系缚——独行如犀角。

Discarding the marks of the householder, like the koviḷāra tree shedding its leaves, the hero who has gone forth cuts the bonds of the household — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

The koviḷāra (a flowering tree that sheds its leaves seasonally) gives the image of a complete, natural letting go — not violent but total. Going forth from the household is described as heroic (vīra): it takes a particular courage to step outside the structures of social identity, to shed the markers of family, status, and belonging.

若遇智慧的同行者，品行善良、坚定不移，克服一切危难，欢喜与他同行。

若能遇到有智慧的同行者，品行善良、坚毅不移，能克服一切危难，那就欢喜地与他同行吧。

If one finds a prudent companion, a fellow wanderer of good conduct and firm resolve, who overcomes all dangers — travel with him, joyful and mindful.

The sutta makes an important qualification: solitude is not the ideal in itself. If a genuinely good companion is available — wise, virtuous, resolute — travelling with them is excellent. The point is not to be alone for its own sake but to avoid the entanglements of companionship that does not support one's practice. The good companion amplifies the path; they do not divert it.

若不遇智慧的同行者，如被击败的国王舍弃其国——独行如犀角。

若找不到这样的同行者，就如同被击败的国王舍弃自己的王国那样果断——独行如犀角。

If one finds no prudent companion, no fellow wanderer of good conduct — like a king who abandons a conquered realm — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

The image of the defeated king is striking: a king who has lost his kingdom does not linger — he leaves decisively, without sentimentality. Companions who do not support the path are not to be clung to out of habit or loneliness. Better to depart cleanly than to remain in company that pulls one away from what matters.

当称赞结伴之乐，此乃真实；但若遇不到胜己或等己之友——独行如犀角。

结伴之乐确实值得称赞，这是真实的。但若找不到胜过自己或与自己相当的朋友——独行如犀角。

We should indeed praise the companionship of good friends — this much is true. But finding no companions superior or equal to oneself — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

Good companionship is genuinely praised — the Buddha elsewhere calls the good friend (kalyāṇamitta) the 'whole of the holy life.' The qualification is purely practical: such friends are rare. The tradition consistently identifies three categories: those superior to us (from whom we learn), those equal to us (with whom we grow), and those inferior (to whom we teach). Without the first two, solitude is preferable to companionship that leads nowhere.

见到精心打造、光彩夺目的黄金手镯，两只在一臂上叮当作响——独行如犀角。

两只精美的黄金手镯戴在同一条臂上，互相碰撞叮当作响——独行如犀角。

Seeing the gleaming, well-wrought golden bracelets — two on one arm, clinking together — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

The bracelet image is elegantly simple: two bracelets on one arm inevitably make noise against each other. One bracelet is silent. This is not a metaphor about the problem of togetherness in the abstract but about the specific friction of two personalities, two agendas, two spiritual trajectories occupying the same space. Conflict is built into the structure — not because people are bad but because they are two.

如此，与他人同处，便会有话语争论；预见此过患——独行如犀角。

与他人同处，话语交锋与争论必然产生；预见到这种过患——独行如犀角。

In this way, with others, there arise disputes of words; seeing this danger ahead — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

The bracelets clinking becomes, in human terms, verbal friction. Words exchanged between two people inevitably generate disagreement — about how to practise, where to go, what to prioritise, how to interpret the teaching. Even between well-meaning practitioners, this friction is real. Seeing it in advance is wisdom, not pessimism.

欲乐的享受，种种多彩，令人愉悦，以种种方式扰乱心——见欲乐之过患——独行如犀角。

感官欲乐，种类繁多，令人愉悦，却以种种方式扰动心念——看清欲乐的过患——独行如犀角。

Sensual pleasures, varied and alluring, sweet in their many forms, disturb the mind in many ways — seeing the danger in sensual pleasures — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

The turn from companionship to sensual pleasure marks a broadening of the sutta's scope. Pleasures are described honestly: varied, alluring, sweet. The teaching is not that they are inherently evil but that they disturb — they agitate the mind, generate craving, and prevent the stillness in which genuine understanding can arise.

此乃我的灾祸，痈疮、苦痛、疾病、箭矢——见欲乐之恐惧——独行如犀角。

感官欲乐是灾祸、是痈疮、是苦痛、是疾病、是深入肉中的箭矢——看清欲乐令人恐惧之处——独行如犀角。

These are my afflictions — a boil, an ulcer, a misfortune, a disease, an arrow — seeing the fear in sensual pleasures — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

Five metaphors for what sensual pleasures actually are beneath their attractive surface: boil (festering beneath the skin), ulcer (open wound that won't heal), misfortune (bringing disaster), disease (self-perpetuating illness), arrow (penetrating, painful, requiring extraction). The accumulation of images is deliberate — each approaches the same truth from a slightly different angle, making the point inescapable.

寒与热、饥与渴、风与日的炙烤，虻与蚊虫——忍受一切——独行如犀角。

寒冷与酷热、饥饿与干渴、风吹日晒、虻蚊叮咬——忍受这一切——独行如犀角。

Cold and heat, hunger and thirst, wind and sun's burning, gadflies and snakes — having endured all these — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

The solitary life in the forest was genuinely hard — not metaphorically but physically. This verse honours that reality without softening it. Cold, heat, thirst, insect bites, exposure: these were the actual conditions of the wandering practitioner's life. Endurance of physical discomfort is a real part of the path, not a romantic abstraction.

如大象舍弃象群，在林中独行游荡，所行随意——独行如犀角。

如同大象离开象群，在林中独自自在游荡，随心所欲地去到任何地方——独行如犀角。

Like a great elephant that has left the herd, wandering freely in the forest, going wherever it wishes — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

The elephant is one of the most powerful animals; it is also deeply social. An elephant that leaves the herd has made a significant choice. Its freedom in the forest is not lonely but sovereign: it goes where it will, eats what nourishes it, rests when it needs to. The image amplifies the rhinoceros horn — another powerful, solitary creature — and shows that the choice of solitude is a natural movement for the strong, not a refuge of the weak.

对于那爱好聚会、欢乐相聚之人，解脱的时候未曾到来；听取太阳亲族之语——独行如犀角。

对于那些喜爱聚会、乐于嬉乐的人，解脱的时机从未到来；听从太阳族（佛陀）的教言——独行如犀角。

For those who delight in gatherings and in pleasurable association, the moment of liberation does not come; heeding the word of the Kinsman of the Sun — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

'Kinsman of the Sun' is an epithet of the Buddha, referencing the Solar lineage claimed by the Sakyan clan. The verse is direct: constant social engagement defers liberation indefinitely. Not because socialising is evil, but because liberation requires a quality of sustained inward attention that a life of continuous social activity cannot support.

已超越见解、已达到确定、已获得道的人，我不用这种方式引导他——独行如犀角。

对于那些已超越种种见解、已获得确定、已证入道的人，不需要用这种方式来引导他——独行如犀角。

Transcending views, having arrived at certainty, having attained the path — such a one is not to be guided in this way — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

A subtle acknowledgement that this teaching is not universal. For one who has already attained the path — who has directly seen the truth, uprooted the deep fetters — the instruction 'wander alone' is no longer needed. They already dwell in the freedom this sutta points toward, whether alone or in company. The teaching is a vehicle; the one who has arrived does not need to keep driving.

不贪、不欺、不渴爱，去除了虚伪，在世间洗尽了烦恼与愚痴——独行如犀角。

不贪婪、不欺骗、不渴爱，去除了虚伪，洗净了世间的烦恼与愚痴——独行如犀角。

Without greed, without deceit, without craving, having shed hypocrisy, with all defilements and delusion washed away — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

A portrait of the purified practitioner: five qualities removed — greed, deceit, craving, hypocrisy, defilement and delusion together. The washing image returns from the Uraga Sutta: defilements are not intrinsic but adherent, and they can be removed. What remains after washing is not nothing — it is the clear, open mind that is the ground of liberation.

避开恶知识，那些不以福祉为目标的人；自身不依附贪婪爱好者——独行如犀角。

远离恶知识，那些不以他人利益为目标的人；自己也不依附于贪婪成性的人——独行如犀角。

Shunning bad companions, those whose goal is not one's welfare — oneself not associating with those addicted to greed — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

The sutta's consistent position: companions matter because association shapes character. Bad companions — those whose goals oppose genuine welfare, those consumed by greed — are not merely neutral presences to be tolerated. They exert a real pull. Shunning them is not snobbery but self-protection of the practice.

结交博学之士，法义精通者，了知义理者，克服疑惑，独行如犀角。

结交博学之人、通晓法义者、明了真义者，克服了种种疑惑——独行如犀角。

Associating with the learned, those well-versed in the Dhamma, those who know the meaning, those who have overcome doubt — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

When good companions are available, seek them actively: the learned (those with broad knowledge), the Dhamma-versed (those with deep doctrinal understanding), the meaning-knowers (those who understand application, not just theory), the doubt-free (those whose practice has resolved the fundamental uncertainties). These four are worth seeking out — but in their absence, proceed alone.

世间的游戏玩乐，对我无所滋养，不含丝毫娱乐；不顾世间庄严——独行如犀角。

世间的游戏娱乐对我毫无滋养，其中也不含真正的快乐；不被世间的庄严华丽所吸引——独行如犀角。

The sports and pleasures of the world are of no nourishment to me, nor is there even a little enjoyment; not heedful of the world's adornments — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

A statement of changed taste rather than moral condemnation. The practitioner does not refuse games and ornaments because they are forbidden but because they have ceased to attract — they offer no real nourishment. This is the natural result of genuine practice: what once appealed simply loses its pull, not through suppression but through a shift in what one finds satisfying.

放下对子女的渴爱，更何况对同伴；如白莲花不为水所染——独行如犀角。

放下对子女的渴爱，更不用说对同伴的执取；如白莲花出于水中而不被水所染——独行如犀角。

Discarding craving for children, and even more for companions — like a white lotus not stained by the water — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

The lotus image — one of the most beloved in Buddhist literature — expresses the quality of non-contamination. The lotus grows rooted in muddy water yet its flower is pristine. The practitioner lives in the world, surrounded by craving and attachment, but is not soiled by them. This is a more refined image than earlier verses: not fleeing the world but being untouched by it while fully within it.

在各方面克服贪欲，心无恶意，超越一切苦乐——独行如犀角。

在各方面克服了贪欲，心中无有恶意，已超越一切苦与乐——独行如犀角。

Having overcome craving in all respects, with no malice in mind, having gone beyond all pleasure and pain — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

Three qualities of full development: craving overcome in all forms (not selectively), malice entirely absent, and equanimity toward both pleasure and pain. 'Gone beyond pleasure and pain' does not mean no experience of either but no reactive identification with either — the mind receives both without being seized by either.

为了实现真正的至善，以利益为目标，克服五种障碍，不走弯路——独行如犀角。

为了真正的至善，以实际的利益为目标，克服五种障碍（五盖），不偏离正道——独行如犀角。

Striving for the highest good, intent on benefit, having overcome the five hindrances, not deviating — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

The five hindrances — sensual desire, ill will, sloth-and-torpor, restlessness-and-worry, and doubt — are the five mental factors that most consistently obstruct meditation and clear seeing. Overcoming them is not a one-time achievement but an ongoing practice of recognition and release. Having overcome them, the path becomes straight.

放下悦意与不悦，离开一切依止，征服一切依附——独行如犀角。

放下悦意与不悦，离开一切依靠，征服一切执取与依附——独行如犀角。

Abandoning the agreeable and disagreeable, leaving all supports, having conquered all attachments — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

The agreeable (piya) and disagreeable (appiya) are the two poles of reactive experience — the things we reach toward and the things we push away. To abandon both is to stand in the middle ground of equanimity, no longer driven by attraction or aversion. 'All supports' (nissaya) points to the various things we lean on for security — people, possessions, identity. Conquering all attachments is the completion of this release.

为了最高的利益，已舍弃欲乐，不障碍任何人，不去伤害众生——独行如犀角。

为了最高的利益，已舍弃感官欲乐，不障碍任何人，不伤害任何众生——独行如犀角。

Having abandoned sensual pleasures for the sake of the highest good, not obstructing anyone, not harming any being — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

The solitary path is not an aggressive one. The practitioner who wanders alone does not do so in competition with or contempt for others. They obstruct no one's path and harm no being. The renunciation of pleasure is not performed to impress others or demonstrate superiority — it is a private transaction between the practitioner and the highest good they seek.

如火燃烧时不回头，斩断贪欲与嗔恨——独行如犀角。

如同燃烧的火焰从不回头，斩断了贪与嗔——独行如犀角。

Like a fire that does not turn back, having cut off passion and aversion — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

Fire moves forward without hesitation or reversal — it does not look back at what it has already consumed, nor does it stop in uncertainty. This image of irreversible forward movement applies to the cutting off of passion and aversion: once genuinely severed, there is no going back to pick them up again. The path moves in one direction.

在愤怒中离开，在嗔恚中离开，在欺骗中离开，在愚痴中离开——独行如犀角。

离开了愤怒，离开了嗔恚，离开了欺骗，离开了愚痴——独行如犀角。

Having gone out from anger, having gone out from aversion, having gone out from delusion, having gone out from craving — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

Four departures, each using the same verb 'going out' — the same word used for nibbāna (the going out of the flame). This is not accidental: each of these departures — from anger, aversion, delusion, craving — is a small nibbāna, a partial extinguishing. The complete wandering alone, this verse implies, is a continuous extinguishing.

抛开善恶，将执取干净去除，清净无染，无有烦恼——独行如犀角。

超越善与恶的对立，彻底去除执取，清净无染，无有任何烦恼——独行如犀角。

Leaving behind both good and bad, having thoroughly shed all clinging, pure, undefiled, with no defilements — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

The instruction to 'leave behind good and bad' is one of the subtler teachings in the sutta. It does not mean to abandon ethical conduct but to release attachment to the identity of 'one who does good.' Even virtue, clung to as a source of pride or identity, is a subtle form of attachment. Complete purity means not holding even the 'good' as a possession of self.

以利益为方向，清净、精进、思维深邃，在诸法中已确立——独行如犀角。

朝向真正的利益，清净、精进、思维深邃，在一切法中已然确立——独行如犀角。

Intent on benefit, pure, energetic, of deep reflection, well-established in all the dhammas — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

Four qualities of the mature practitioner: intentionality toward genuine benefit (not superficial comfort), purity (freedom from defilement), energy (active, sustained engagement), and depth of reflection (not surface-level understanding). 'Well-established in all the dhammas' means one who knows the teachings not merely intellectually but as lived reality.

如狮子不被声音所惊，如风不被网所捕，如莲花不被水所染——独行如犀角。

如狮子不被声音所惊扰，如风不被网所阻拦，如莲花不被水所沾染——独行如犀角。

Like a lion not startled by sounds, like the wind not caught in a net, like a lotus not defiled by water — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

Three images of freedom, each from a different element. The lion is unmoved by noise — its equanimity is robust, not fragile. The wind passes through any obstacle — it cannot be trapped. The lotus, already established in the verse about children, returns here as the signature image of purity within the world. Together, the three capture the practitioner's fearlessness, freedom, and non-contamination.

如狮子以牙齿征服强大的兽类，统率群兽而游行，在偏远处栖息——独行如犀角。

如狮子以其利齿制服强敌，统领诸兽，却栖居于偏远之处——独行如犀角。

Like a lion, the conqueror, who has overcome all comers with its teeth — the king of beasts who roams alone in remote places — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

The lion image is extended: this is not a weak or frightened creature retreating from society. The lion is the strongest animal in its world, yet it roams alone in remote places by nature, not by defeat. The solitary practitioner's choice of solitude comes from strength and self-sufficiency, not from social failure or timidity.

时时修习慈、悲、喜、舍与解脱，不被世界所阻碍——独行如犀角。

时时修习慈、悲、喜、舍四梵住与内心解脱，不被世界的任何事物所阻碍——独行如犀角。

Cultivating loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, and equanimity in season, not obstructed by anything in the world — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

The four brahmavihāras — loving-kindness, compassion, sympathetic joy, equanimity — are not the exclusive property of social life. They are boundless precisely because they are directed toward all beings, not restricted to one's companions. The solitary practitioner cultivates them toward the whole world, unimpeded by personal entanglements that might restrict or distort their scope.

放弃贪与嗔，舍弃痴，去除诸结，不畏死——独行如犀角。

放弃了贪，放弃了嗔，舍弃了痴，去除了一切系缚，不惧怕死亡——独行如犀角。

Having abandoned passion, aversion, and delusion, having broken the fetters, undisturbed at death — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

The three roots of unwholesome action — passion (lobha), aversion (dosa), delusion (moha) — appear here as a summary of all that has been released. With these gone and the fetters broken, death itself loses its sting. The practitioner who no longer clings to self has no death to fear, because there is no fixed thing to be lost.

有人为利益而结交，如今友谊因利益而存在；无求而交之友，今日难觅——独行如犀角。

人们为了利益而结交，如今的友谊大多建立在利益之上；不求回报的纯粹之友，今日已难寻——独行如犀角。

People associate for profit; nowadays friendship is built on gain. Friends who want nothing are rare today — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

A sociological observation as relevant now as in the 5th century BCE: most friendships are transactional, maintained by mutual benefit. When the benefit ends, so often does the friendship. This is not cynicism but realism. Genuine friendship — the kind the sutta earlier praised as worth seeking — is rare. In its absence, honest solitude is preferable to the performance of mutual interest.

在世间察知自身之利，不谋害他人，独行如犀角。

在世间洞察自身真正的利益，不伤害他人——独行如犀角。

Knowing one's own welfare in the world, not harming others — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

The penultimate verse combines inward and outward orientation: understanding what actually benefits oneself (not what merely pleases), and ensuring that this pursuit causes no harm to others. The solitary path is not self-centred in the pejorative sense — it does no injury. It is simply a path of clear, unobstructed self-knowledge.

如林中大象舍弃象群，不受系缚——独行如犀角。

如同林中大象离开象群，自由自在，不受任何系缚——独行如犀角。

Like a great elephant in the forest that has left the herd, free and unbound — wander alone like a rhinoceros horn.

The sutta closes with the elephant image it introduced in verse 18 — a deliberate return that frames the whole collection. Where verse 18 emphasised the elephant's freedom to go wherever it wishes, this final verse emphasises its unboundedness: free, not fettered to the herd. The rhinoceros horn is the last word: single, pointed, moving forward without hesitation or return. This is the sutta's final image of the fully liberated mind.

总结 · Summary

犀角经是《经集》中篇幅最长、也最具诗意的经典之一，四十一颂，以同一个叠句贯穿始终：「独行如犀角」。犀牛角独一、笔直、向前——是一心专注、无所依附、无所回顾的完美象征。经文并不反对友谊或同伴，而是明确指出：真正有益的同伴极为难得，而错误的同伴则会带来爱恋、争论、分心与牵绊。狮子、大象、风、莲花——每一个意象都从不同角度描绘同一种自由：在世间中而不为世间所染，强大而不依附，慈悲而不系缚，孤独而不孤立。这样的独行，不是逃避，而是最彻底的在场。

The Rhinoceros Horn Sutta is the longest and perhaps most poetically sustained text in Vagga 1 — forty-one verses built around a single refrain that returns with the regularity of breath. The rhinoceros horn is a perfect image precisely because it is anatomically simple: one horn, not two, not branched. It points in one direction. The sutta does not oppose companionship in principle — it explicitly praises the good companion and says to travel joyfully with them if found. Its argument is statistical and practical: such companions are rare, while the entanglements of ordinary companionship are constant. The lion, elephant, wind, and lotus offer four approaches to the same freedom — power that does not need a group, movement that cannot be trapped, presence that is not contaminated. What the sutta describes is not misanthropy but a radical reorientation of the self's centre of gravity: no longer organised around others' approval, the social web, or the comfort of belonging, but around the unimpeded movement toward what is genuinely real.
