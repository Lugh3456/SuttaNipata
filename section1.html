第一品·第一经 · 蛇经

经集 · Sutta Nipata

目录 Contents 第一品·第二经 →

如人以良药解蛇毒之蔓延，比丘灭起之嗔，舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。如人入湖以水灭火，比丘断尽一切贪欲，舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。如奔流之水冲垮芦苇细桥，比丘截断慢心，舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。如人遍寻优昙花树而不可得，比丘遍观诸有亦无实质，舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。内心无嗔恚，超越生死之流，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。洗净一切烦恼污垢，不再返回此世，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。竭尽妄想之流，不令再度泛滥，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。无所执取，不被此岸彼岸所系缚，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。无所执取，不被名与色所系缚，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。放下一切担负，不再拾取，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。于此世彼世，无有渴爱可得，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。于诸有中，无有渴爱生起，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。于欲乐无渴求，解脱一切系缚，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。断除种种戏论，心无所依附，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。无过多亦无过少，超越一切诸流，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。舍弃渴爱，不执取此世，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。无有余残，彻底放舍一切烦恼，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。

逐句解释

如人以良药解蛇毒之蔓延，比丘灭起之嗔，舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。

就像人用良药制止蛇毒的蔓延，比丘能在嗔恚生起之际将其息灭——这样的比丘放舍此生，如同蛇蜕去旧皮。

As with herbs one checks the spreading of snake venom, a monk who checks anger as it arises — he sheds this life, as the snake its worn-out skin.

The sutta opens with its controlling image: the snake. Here it is venom — anger spreading through the mind the way poison spreads through the body. The practitioner, like a skilled physician, acts at the moment of arising, before the spread takes hold. The refrain that closes every verse — shedding life as the snake sheds its skin — points to the complete release of clinging, not physical death but the death of the identifying self.

如人入湖以水灭火，比丘断尽一切贪欲，舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。

就像人走入湖中将火浇灭，比丘彻底断尽一切贪欲——这样的比丘放舍此生，如同蛇蜕去旧皮。

As with water one quenches a fire in a forest pool, a monk who quenches passion without remainder — he sheds this life, as the snake its worn-out skin.

Where the first verse addressed anger, this one addresses passion — the pull of desire. Water quenches fire completely: not suppressed, not managed, but extinguished. The emphasis on 'without remainder' is deliberate; partial quenching is not liberation.

如奔流之水冲垮芦苇细桥，比丘截断慢心，舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。

就像奔涌的洪水冲垮细弱的芦苇桥，比丘彻底截断我慢——这样的比丘放舍此生，如同蛇蜕去旧皮。

As a flood undermines the fragile bridge of reeds, a monk who has fully cut off conceit — he sheds this life, as the snake its worn-out skin.

Conceit (māna) is the persistent sense of self-comparison: I am better than, equal to, or inferior to others. The reed bridge — plausible, functional for a while — collapses the moment real force meets it. Conceit is exactly that: a structure that looks like solid ground but cannot hold. The practitioner who has cut it through is on firmer footing than most.

如人遍寻优昙花树而不可得，比丘遍观诸有亦无实质，舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。

就像人遍寻无花果树上的花而无所获，比丘遍观一切存有，也找不到任何实质——这样的比丘放舍此生，如同蛇蜕去旧皮。

As one who looks for flowers on a fig tree finds no essence there, a monk who looks through all existence and finds no core — he sheds this life, as the snake its worn-out skin.

The fig produces no visible flower — the flowers are hidden inside the fruit. Searching for them externally yields nothing. Existence is like this: we search for a fixed, substantial self and come up empty. Not because nothing is there, but because there is no 'thing' to find — only process, flux, interdependence. This seeing dissolves the ground of clinging.

内心无嗔恚，超越生死之流，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。

内心毫无嗔恚，已超越了生死的洪流——这样的比丘放舍此生，如同蛇蜕去旧皮。

Carrying no anger within, gone beyond all birth and death — the monk sheds this life, as the snake its worn-out skin.

Having dealt with anger in the first verse as something to extinguish at arising, the sutta now describes the accomplished state: no anger carried within. The fire is not just quenched at each episode — there is nothing left to ignite. This corresponds to crossing beyond the cycle of conditioned existence.

洗净一切烦恼污垢，不再返回此世，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。

洗涤净尽一切烦恼污垢，不会再度返回此世——这样的比丘放舍此生，如同蛇蜕去旧皮。

Having washed off all defilements, not returning to this world again — the monk sheds this life, as the snake its worn-out skin.

The image of washing is used carefully: defilements are not inherent to the mind but adherent to it — accumulated, not fundamental. A thoroughly washed cloth is clean again; a thoroughly purified mind does not accumulate the conditions that would generate a new life in the cycle of rebirth.

竭尽妄想之流，不令再度泛滥，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。

已枯竭妄想与戏论的洪流，不再让它泛滥成灾——这样的比丘放舍此生，如同蛇蜕去旧皮。

Having dried up the flood of proliferating thought, with no further flooding — the monk sheds this life, as the snake its worn-out skin.

Papañca — mental proliferation — is the habitual tendency of the unguarded mind to elaborate on every perception, spinning out associations, narratives, fears, and desires. The practitioner who has dried up this stream is not unable to think clearly; they are free from the compulsive, self-referential commentary that normally accompanies experience.

无所执取，不被此岸彼岸所系缚，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。

无所执取，既不被此岸（感官世界）所缚，也不被彼岸（精神理想）所缚——这样的比丘放舍此生，如同蛇蜕去旧皮。

Taking up nothing, bound neither to the near shore nor to the far — the monk sheds this life, as the snake its worn-out skin.

The 'near shore' is the realm of sensual existence; the 'far shore' is sometimes identified with the formless realms or even with wrong views about liberation. Freedom is not clinging to either. This is a subtle teaching: even the aspiration toward liberation, if grasped at, becomes another form of bondage.

无所执取，不被名与色所系缚，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。

无所执取，不被名（心理现象）与色（物质现象）所系缚——这样的比丘放舍此生，如同蛇蜕去旧皮。

Taking up nothing, not bound to name-and-form — the monk sheds this life, as the snake its worn-out skin.

Name-and-form (nāmarūpa) is the basic dyad of mental and physical existence — what we ordinarily call 'a person.' Clinging to this composite as 'myself' is the fundamental movement of unawakened life. The practitioner described here is not annihilated but simply no longer identified with and grasping at this conditioned arising.

放下一切担负，不再拾取，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。

放下了一切重担，不再重新拾起——这样的比丘放舍此生，如同蛇蜕去旧皮。

Having laid down every burden and taken nothing up again — the monk sheds this life, as the snake its worn-out skin.

The five aggregates (form, feeling, perception, mental formations, consciousness) are traditionally called 'burdens.' We spend enormous energy carrying them, managing them, protecting them, adorning them. Setting them down does not mean ceasing to exist — it means ceasing to haul them around as the core of an identity that must be defended.

于此世彼世，无有渴爱可得，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。

在此世与彼世，都找不到任何渴爱的痕迹——这样的比丘放舍此生，如同蛇蜕去旧皮。

In whom no craving is to be found, neither here nor there — the monk sheds this life, as the snake its worn-out skin.

Craving (taṇhā) reaches in two directions: toward this life and its pleasures, and toward some imagined future life or state. Freedom from craving is freedom in both directions simultaneously. There is no 'safe' object of craving — even virtuous objects, if clung to with craving, bind.

于诸有中，无有渴爱生起，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。

在一切形式的存有中，都不生起对存有的渴爱——这样的比丘放舍此生，如同蛇蜕去旧皮。

In whom no craving for any form of existence arises — the monk sheds this life, as the snake its worn-out skin.

Bhava-taṇhā — craving for existence itself — is one of the three forms of craving identified in the Second Noble Truth. It is the deep, pre-reflective urge to continue, to persist, to be. The practitioner described here no longer experiences this impulse, not because existence has been suppressed but because its nature has been thoroughly understood.

于欲乐无渴求，解脱一切系缚，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。

对感官欲乐毫无渴求，从一切系缚中解脱——这样的比丘放舍此生，如同蛇蜕去旧皮。

Having no craving for sensual pleasures, freed from every bond — the monk sheds this life, as the snake its worn-out skin.

Kāma-taṇhā — craving for sensual pleasure — is the most obvious form of binding desire. Freedom from it is not cold indifference to life's experiences but the absence of compulsive reaching and clinging. The freed mind can experience the world's sensory richness without being enslaved by it.

断除种种戏论，心无所依附，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。

断除了各种思维戏论，心不再依附任何事物——这样的比丘放舍此生，如同蛇蜕去旧皮。

Having abandoned the proliferations of thought, with no reliance — the monk sheds this life, as the snake its worn-out skin.

Proliferating thought (papañca) and reliance (nissaya) are paired here: the mind that elaborates endlessly also clings — to views, to outcomes, to the narratives it constructs. Stopping the proliferation and releasing the reliance are two aspects of the same movement: coming back to direct experience without the overlay of habitual interpretation.

无过多亦无过少，超越一切诸流，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。

不过分亦不匮乏，超越了一切泛滥的洪流——这样的比丘放舍此生，如同蛇蜕去旧皮。

With neither excess nor deficiency, having crossed over all the floods — the monk sheds this life, as the snake its worn-out skin.

The Middle Way is implicit here: not excess (indulgence) and not deficiency (harsh austerity). The four floods — sensuality, existence, views, and ignorance — are the great currents that carry beings through the cycle of rebirth. Crossing them does not require superhuman effort but balanced, sustained practice.

舍弃渴爱，不执取此世，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。

舍弃渴爱，不再执取此世——这样的比丘放舍此生，如同蛇蜕去旧皮。

Having cast off craving, not clinging to this world — the monk sheds this life, as the snake its worn-out skin.

Craving and clinging are sequential: craving reaches toward the object; clinging grasps it and refuses to let go. Abandoning craving cuts the sequence at its root. 'This world' — the world of the senses, the world of experience — is not rejected but released. The monk is still present in it; he simply no longer clings.

无有余残，彻底放舍一切烦恼，比丘舍此生命，如蛇脱旧皮。

毫无余残，彻底放舍了一切烦恼——这样的比丘放舍此生，如同蛇蜕去旧皮。

With no remainder, having entirely relinquished all defilements — the monk sheds this life, as the snake its worn-out skin.

The final verse closes the sutta with emphasis on completeness: 'no remainder.' Partial release is progress, but the sutta points to the final, total shedding — the complete release that gives the text its name and central image. As a snake does not keep part of its old skin, the fully liberated practitioner carries nothing of the old accumulations forward.

总结 · Summary

蛇经以十七颂、反复出现的叠句，描绘了一位彻底解脱的修行者形象。每一颂指向一种已被放下的烦恼——嗔恚、贪欲、我慢、执取、戏论、渴爱——然后以同一个意象作结：如蛇蜕去旧皮。蛇蜕皮并非死亡，而是完全的更新：旧皮彻底脱落，没有任何留存。经文用这个自然界的事实，指向心灵的彻底解放——不是压制烦恼，而是将其连根拔除，令其无从再生。

The Uraga Sutta builds its teaching entirely through a single sustained image: the snake shedding its skin. Across seventeen verses, each describing one quality of the liberated practitioner — anger extinguished, passion quenched, conceit cut, craving dried up, proliferation stilled — the refrain returns unchanged: he sheds this life as the snake its worn-out skin. The snake does not partially shed; it does not regret what falls away. The skin is gone completely, and the snake moves forward renewed. This is the sutta's picture of liberation: not suppression or management of defilements but their thorough, remainder-free release, leaving nothing of the old accumulation to carry forward.
