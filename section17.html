第二品·第五经 · 针毛经

经集 · Sutta Nipata

【针毛夜叉】沙门，你害怕吗？ 【世尊】朋友，我不害怕你。你能做什么？ 【针毛夜叉】我要问你一个问题，沙门。如果你不回答，我将使你的心神错乱，或者撕裂你的胸膛，或者抓住你的双脚，把你扔到恒河对岸。 【世尊】朋友，在这个世间，包括诸神、梵天、沙门、婆罗门在内，我看不见任何人能使我心神错乱，撕裂我的胸膛，或者抓住双脚把我扔过去。但是，朋友，请问你的问题。 【针毛夜叉】贪欲与嗔恚从何而来？不喜与喜悦从何而来？使心念生起的念头——请告诉我，它们从何处升起？ 【世尊】贪欲与嗔恚从自身升起；不喜与喜悦从自身升起；使心念生起的念头——它们从自身升起。 如孩子由身体升起，如攀藤由树生长；它们根植于欲望，如树林中的藤蔓缠绕树木。 了解这个起源的人——他们赶走那些欲望，穿越了难以逾越的洪流，不再回来，再度出生。

逐句解释

【针毛夜叉】沙门，你害怕吗？……我将使你的心神错乱，或者撕裂你的胸膛，或者抓住你的双脚，把你扔到恒河对岸。

针毛（Sūciloma，「针毛」，毛如针刺）是一位凶猛的夜叉，经文开篇便以威胁建立张力。他的问话「你害怕吗？」是一种测试。佛陀的回应「我不害怕你，你能做什么？」不是挑衅，而是真实的内在状态——一位彻底离欲、心无所恐的人，对外在威胁有如实的平静。夜叉随后的威胁（使人心乱、撕裂胸膛、扔过恒河）描绘了夜叉在传统中所具有的超自然力量，而佛陀以无畏回应。

Sūciloma the yakkha: Are you afraid, ascetic? … I will disorder your mind, split open your chest, or grab you by the feet and throw you across the Ganges.

Sūciloma ('needle-hair') is a fierce yakkha, a non-human being of dangerous power. The opening question — 'Are you afraid?' — is a test of the Buddha's composure. The threats that follow are not mere bluster; in the cosmology of the Sutta Nipata, yakkhas are capable of the harms they name. The Buddha's response — 'I am not afraid of you; what can you do?' — is not bravado but the description of an actual state: a mind liberated from fear because it is liberated from craving. What yakkhas and storms and threats share is the capacity to disturb; the liberated mind is undisturbable not through suppression but through the absence of whatever they would disturb.

【针毛夜叉】贪欲与嗔恚从何而来？不喜与喜悦从何而来？使心念生起的念头——请告诉我，它们从何处升起？

威胁之后，针毛提出了一个哲学问题，这是经集中对话型经文的典型转折：凶猛的问者最终显露出对真理的渴望。问题触及心理苦的根源：贪欲（rāga）、嗔恚（dosa）、不喜（arati，不满）、喜悦（rati，沉溺的喜悦）、心念（vitakka，思虑与寻）——这些精神现象从何处生起？这是一个深刻的问题，指向整个缘起论的核心。

Sūciloma: From where do lust and aversion arise? From where do boredom and delight arise? From where do thoughts arise to obsess the mind?

The turn from threat to philosophical question is characteristic of the Sutta Nipata's encounter narratives: the challenging interlocutor turns out to have a genuine question. Sūciloma's question is precise and important — it asks for the origin (yato) of the mental states that drive suffering: lust (rāga), aversion (dosa), boredom/discontent (arati), sensual delight (rati), and the obsessive proliferation of thoughts (vitakka). This is the question of dependent origination applied specifically to mental experience: these states arise — but from where?

【世尊】贪欲与嗔恚从自身升起……如孩子由身体升起，如攀藤由树生长；它们根植于欲望，如树林中的藤蔓缠绕树木。

佛陀的回答是「从自身升起」（attasambhūta，从自身产生）——不是从外部强加，不是神灵或命运所赐，而是从这个身心本身产生的。两个比喻阐明这一点：孩子由父母的身体升起（内在生产）；攀藤由树自身升起（寄生于自身）。欲望（kāma）是这一切的根基——如树木支撑整个藤蔓网络。这是一个既深刻又令人解脱的洞见：苦的根源在自身，而不在外部，因此改变的可能性也在自身。

The Blessed One: Lust and aversion arise from oneself … as a child arises from a body, as a creeper grows from a tree. They are rooted in desire, as vines entangle trees in a forest.

Attasambhūta — 'arising from oneself' — is the Buddha's answer: these mental states are not imposed from outside, not given by fate or gods, but produced by this very mind-body complex itself. The two similes work in parallel: a child arises from and is sustained by the parent's body; a climbing vine arises from and is sustained by the tree it climbs. Desire (kāma) is the substrate from which all these states grow. The teaching is both sobering and liberating: sobering because we cannot blame external forces for our suffering; liberating because the source being internal means the solution is also internal.

了解这个起源的人——他们赶走那些欲望，穿越了难以逾越的洪流，不再回来，再度出生。

结语以实践与解脱作结：了解这一起源的人能够「赶走」（pajahananti，驱除）那些欲望，穿越「难以逾越的洪流」（ogha，瀑流，指欲流、有流、见流、无明流四种束缚众生的洪流），达到「不再回来，再度出生」（na te jātipunabbhavaṃ）——不再投生。「不再投生」是阿罗汉果的描述，是整个修行路径的终点。了解苦的起源，是解脱的开端。

Those who understand this origin — they drive out those desires, they cross the flood difficult to cross, and do not return to birth again.

Understanding (pajānanti) the origin of these mental states is presented as the gateway to liberation. Those who understand can 'drive out' (pajahananti) the desires — not suppress them but uproot them through insight into their arising. The 'flood difficult to cross' (ogha) refers to the four floods in Pali cosmology: sensual desire, existence, views, and ignorance. These are what keep beings in the cycle of rebirth. Crossing them — not returning to birth again — is the description of the arahant's final liberation. The sutta traces the entire arc from the question 'where does suffering arise?' to 'how does one become free?' in a handful of verses.

总结 · Summary

针毛经是一首以戏剧性对话为框架的短小而深刻的经典。凶猛的夜叉针毛以威胁开篇，却以哲学问题结束：贪欲、嗔恚、不满、沉溺的喜悦、迷惑人心的念头——这些从何而来？佛陀的回答是：从自身升起，根植于欲望，如攀藤缠绕宿主之树。这个洞见既沉重（我们是自身苦的制造者）又解脱（因此解脱的可能性也在自身）。了解这一起源的人能够驱除欲望，穿越四种洪流，不再投生——这是阿罗汉果的简洁描述。经文以「不害怕」开始，以「不再回来」结束：无畏与解脱，是同一条路的两端。

The Sūciloma Sutta uses a dramatic frame — the threatening yakkha — to deliver a compact teaching on the origin of mental suffering. The yakkha's philosophical question (where do lust, aversion, boredom, delight, and obsessive thought arise?) receives a precise answer: from oneself, rooted in desire, like vines growing from the tree that hosts them. This is both a sobering and liberating diagnosis: the source of suffering is internal, which means so is the possibility of liberation. Those who understand this origin can uproot desire and cross the four floods — the conditions that perpetuate rebirth — arriving at final liberation. The sutta opens with fearlessness and closes with freedom from rebirth: the two are expressions of the same realisation.
