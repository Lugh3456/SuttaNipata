第四品·第六经 · 老经

经集 · Sutta Nipata

以下经文据汉译《义足经》（T0198，支谦译，三国·吴）

命短不可久，寿尽必当终—— 老人所苦者，此苦无能免。 见此必死相，当弃世间乐—— 为求无死道，牟尼所行道。 人之将死时，亲属来围绕—— 哭泣而哀号，各自求活命。 是故有智者，见苦如实知—— 所爱必当离，此世间法则。 人见所爱者，终不能守护—— 如王护国土，终为他所夺。 命如水中泡，盛者必当衰—— 贪著诸有为，必当遭苦恼。 早起当精勤，莫以今日缓—— 死王无慈悲，不待懈怠者。 见老见病死，是三苦相续—— 智者知无常，弃欲向涅槃。 若人能如此，舍离欲爱著—— 死时无忧惧，意安如涅槃。

逐句解释

命短不可久，寿尽必当终——老人所苦者，此苦无能免。见此必死相，当弃世间乐——为求无死道，牟尼所行道。

老经（Jarā Sutta，jarā = 老年、衰老）在八偈品中风格异于其他各经：它不以见解批判为中心，而以死亡无常的直观呈现为主。「命短不可久，寿尽必当终」——这是最朴素的无常教导：生命是短暂的，死亡是必然的，老人的苦（衰老、病痛、死亡的临近）是每个人都无法免除的命运。「见此必死相，当弃世间乐」：真正见到这个必然性——不是知道它，而是真切地看见——就会自然生起出离心。「为求无死道」（amata，不死之道，即涅槃）：牟尼的道路是走向「不死」（amata，也译「甘露」）的道路。

Life is short and cannot last long; when the span is exhausted, it must end — the suffering of the aged is this: no one can be exempt. Seeing this sign of inevitable death, one should abandon worldly pleasures — to seek the deathless path is the path walked by the sage.

The Jarā Sutta differs from the surrounding Aṭṭhakavagga suttas in its register: instead of the analytic critique of view-clinging, it offers a direct contemplation of aging and death as the experiential basis for the renunciant path. 'Seeing this sign of inevitable death' invokes the classical Buddhist contemplation: the direct, visceral recognition of mortality — not abstract knowledge but the kind of seeing that changes the quality of one's engagement with pleasure. The 'deathless path' (amata-magga) is one of the Pali Canon's oldest epithets for the path to Nibbāna — the destination reached by abandoning what is subject to decay.

人之将死时，亲属来围绕——哭泣而哀号，各自求活命。是故有智者，见苦如实知——所爱必当离，此世间法则。

临终的场景：亲属围绕、哭泣哀号——但即使在这个时刻，「各自求活命」，每个人都面对着自己的死亡。这个观察与箭经（Sn 3.8）的教导呼应：在死亡面前，一切社会关系的保护网络都失效，每个人必须单独面对。「所爱必当离，此世间法则」（vipariṇāmadhamma，变异法，一切所爱之物都有变异分离的本质）——这是智者「见苦如实知」的核心内容：不是悲观，而是如实地看见「所爱皆有离」的世间本质。

When a person is about to die, relatives gather around, weeping and wailing — each seeking their own life. Therefore the wise one, seeing suffering as it truly is, knows: what is loved must be parted from — this is the law of the world.

The deathbed scene isolates each person in their individual mortality despite the crowd: relatives weep, but each is also facing their own death ('seeking their own life'). The 'law of the world' (vipariṇāmadhamma — the nature of change) is the specific content of wise seeing: not pessimism but accurate perception of impermanence. What is loved will be parted from — not as a tragic accident but as the inherent nature of everything that arises in dependence on conditions. Seeing this 'as it truly is' (yathābhūtaṃ) is the foundation of disenchantment (nibbidā) and the beginning of the turn toward liberation.

早起当精勤，莫以今日缓——死王无慈悲，不待懈怠者。

老经中最具紧迫感的颂：「死王无慈悲，不待懈怠者」——死亡（Yama，死王）不等待懈怠的人，不给人「明天再开始修行」的宽限。「早起当精勤，莫以今日缓」：urgency是这一颂的情感基调——不是恐惧，而是清醒的紧迫感：修行的时间窗口是有限的，今日的懈怠就是对明日死亡的无准备。这个主题与起立经（Sn 2.10）的「箭已射入、莫待懈怠」呼应，是整部经集中反复出现的精进主题。

Rise early and be diligent — do not delay today. The king of death has no compassion and does not wait for the negligent.

The urgency of this verse is the Jarā Sutta's most emotionally direct moment: Yama (the god of death, 'king of death') is characterised by the complete absence of accommodation. The teaching is not fear-based but pragmatic: the window for practice is finite, and Māra (or Yama) does not grant extensions to those who intend to begin tomorrow. This connects to the Uṭṭhāna Sutta (Sn 2.10) — 'stand up, the arrow is already in your heart' — and to the final words of the Pali Canon at the parinibbāna: 'all conditioned things are impermanent; strive with diligence.' The contemplation of death in the Jarā Sutta is not morbid but activating: see clearly, then move.

见老见病死，是三苦相续——智者知无常，弃欲向涅槃。若人能如此，舍离欲爱著——死时无忧惧，意安如涅槃。

老经以三苦（老、病、死）的观察为基础，引向两个结论：第一，智者知无常，弃欲向涅槃（解脱的认知基础）；第二，「死时无忧惧，意安如涅槃」——在活着的时候舍离执著的人，在死的时候心境安稳，「如涅槃」（与涅槃的安稳相似）。这最后一颂以一种罕见的温柔作结：死时无忧惧，意安如涅槃——不是对死后涅槃的承诺，而是对活着时修行的人，死亡本身将以一种安稳的方式呈现。这是整个大品中最接近「好死」描述的颂。

Seeing aging, illness, and death — these three sufferings in sequence — the wise one knows impermanence and abandons desire toward Nibbāna. If a person can do this, releasing the clinging of love and desire — at death there is no worry or fear, the mind is peaceful, like Nibbāna.

The closing verses bring the Jarā Sutta to its gentle conclusion: the sequence of the three marks of existence (aging, illness, death) observed clearly produces the cognitive shift toward disenchantment and liberation. The final image — dying with a mind peaceful 'like Nibbāna' — is the sutta's most distinctive contribution: it does not promise the attainment of Nibbāna as a post-mortem destination but describes the quality of dying for one who has, in life, released attachment. The death is peaceful not because of what happens afterward but because of what was released before. The Jarā Sutta is the Aṭṭhakavagga's most humanly consoling text — the teaching on death that ends in stillness rather than analysis.

总结 · Summary

老经在八偈品中风格独特：它不以见解批判为中心，而以死亡无常的直观观察为基础，呈现从「见老病死」到「弃欲向涅槃」的转化路径。经文描述临终场景（亲属围绕哭泣，「各自求活命」），提醒「所爱必当离，此世间法则」，以「死王无慈悲，不待懈怠者」唤起修行的紧迫感。最后以一个温柔的许诺作结：在活着时舍离执著的人，「死时无忧惧，意安如涅槃」。老经是八偈品最人性化的经典——它不分析见解，而直接面对每个人都知道的现实：我们会老，会病，会死——并将这个现实转化为修行的动力与最终的安慰。

The Jarā Sutta stands apart from the surrounding Aṭṭhakavagga texts in its emotional register. Rather than the analytic deconstruction of view-clinging, it offers direct contemplation of aging and death as the experiential basis for renunciation. The deathbed scene, the reminder that what is loved must be parted from, the urgency of 'the king of death does not wait' — these are addressed to the human being who knows, abstractly, that they will die but has not yet let that knowledge land. The closing verse — dying with a mind peaceful like Nibbāna — does not promise a post-mortem destination but describes a quality of dying available to those who release attachment in life. The Jarā Sutta is the Aṭṭhakavagga's most humanly tender text: it meets mortality directly and finds, in the meeting, both urgency and peace.
