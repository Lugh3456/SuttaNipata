第五品·第十一经 · 闍兔甘尼经

经集 · Sutta Nipata · 彼岸道品

← 第五品·第十经 目录 Contents 第五品·第十二经 →

【闍兔甘尼问】 我听说，英雄中的英雄， 你已离欲—— 来问你，洪流的渡越者， 告诉我无欲的寂静。 世尊，你看见了什么—— 告诉我—— 因为你已渡越了欲望的洪流， 以你的眼，我想了解。 【世尊答】 闍兔甘尼，压制欲望的渴望—— 看见出离为安全—— 没有什么可执取，没有什么可拥有—— 不要让欲望带你走入轮回。 过去的，让它枯竭—— 未来的，不要让它生长—— 如果你不执取现在—— 你将平静地行走于世间。 婆罗门，对名与身—— 你的渴求已完全消尽—— 如此，他在此世间不会再次进入 老与死的领域。

逐句解释

压制欲望的渴望——看见出离为安全——没有什么可执取，没有什么可拥有——不要让欲望带你走入轮回。

闍兔甘尼经（Jatukaṇṇī Sutta）的提问者已听说佛陀「已离欲」——他来问的不是理论，而是「无欲的寂静是什么感觉？你看见了什么？」世尊的回答以「压制欲望的渴望」（kāmesu vinaya gedhaṃ）开始——不是压抑欲望本身，而是压制「对欲望的渴望」（对欲乐的渴求心）。「看见出离为安全」：出离（nekkhamma）被视为安全之处，而非剥夺。「没有什么可执取，没有什么可拥有」——再次呼应迦波经的「无执取」主题。

Suppress the craving for sensual pleasures — see renunciation as safety — nothing to be grasped, nothing to be possessed — do not let desire lead you into the round of existence.

Jatukaṇṇī has heard that the Buddha has overcome desire and comes to ask what that actually looks like — 'what do you see from where you stand?' The answer begins with a subtle distinction: suppress not desire itself but 'the craving for desires' (gedhaṃ — the yearning, the pull toward sensual pleasure). 'See renunciation as safety' — the framing of nekkhamma as safety rather than deprivation is central: renunciation is not a loss but a refuge. The echo of the Kappa Sutta's 'nothing to grasp, nothing to possess' confirms a consistent vocabulary of non-grasping across the Pārāyanavagga's second half.

过去的，让它枯竭——未来的，不要让它生长——如果你不执取现在——你将平静地行走于世间。

这是彼岸道品对「三时间的不执取」的最简洁表达：「过去的，让它枯竭」——不滋养对过去的回味与执恋；「未来的，不要让它生长」——不让对未来的期望与渴望发展；「如果你不执取现在」——对当下的感知与体验也不执取。三时间的完全不执取，导向「平静地行走于世间」——不是离开世间，而是在世间中以平静而行。这个指令与第57经（帝须弥勒，Sn 5.3）的「过去的已放弃，未来的不执取，现在的不留恋」遥相呼应。

What is past — let it wither — what is future — do not let it grow — if you do not grasp at the present — you will walk in the world in peace.

The three-time formula for non-grasping recurs here with particular clarity: let past wither (do not feed nostalgia or regret), do not let future grow (do not cultivate expectation or craving), do not grasp the present (do not cling to current experience). The result is 'walking in the world in peace' — not withdrawal from the world but a quality of movement within it. This echoes the Tissametteyya Sutta (Sn 5.3)'s 'past is given up, future not grasped, present not lingered in' — both suttas confirm that the Pārāyanavagga understands liberation as a temporal as well as a perceptual quality: non-grasping across the three times, not only across the four floods.

对名与身——你的渴求已完全消尽——如此，他在此世间不会再次进入老与死的领域。

「名与身」（nāmarūpa）是佛教分析人存在的基本框架——精神（名）与物质（身）的结合。「对名与身的渴求已完全消尽」：对这个身心复合体的渴求消尽，就是解脱的具体内容。「不会再次进入老与死的领域」——渴求是轮回再生的燃料；渴求消尽，再生的条件消尽，「不会再次进入老与死」就是自然的结果。这个结尾将整首经的主题收拢：无欲→无执取→名与身渴求消尽→不再进入轮回。

For one in whom the craving for name-and-form is completely exhausted — in this very world, such a person does not enter again the domain of aging and death.

'Name-and-form' (nāmarūpa) is the standard Pali analysis of personal existence — the psycho-physical compound. Craving for name-and-form is the craving that perpetuates conditioned existence: the desire for a body, a mind, a self-experience to continue. When this craving is 'completely exhausted' (parikkhīṇā), the fuel for renewed existence is spent. 'Does not enter the domain of aging and death' — the sutta closes by connecting the instruction (suppress craving for sensual pleasure, practise non-grasping across the three times) to its consequence: the ending of the round of aging and death. The Jatukaṇṇī Sutta is compact but complete: craving → non-grasping across three times → exhaustion of name-and-form craving → liberation from the aging-death cycle.

总结 · Summary

闍兔甘尼经以「压制欲望的渴望」开始，以「不再进入老与死的领域」结束，中间包含彼岸道品对三时间不执取的最简洁表达：「过去的，让它枯竭；未来的，不要让它生长；如果你不执取现在——你将平静地行走于世间」。这个三时间的不执取，加上对名与身渴求的消尽，是解脱的具体内容。闍兔甘尼经是彼岸道品中结构最完整的小经之一：问（无欲的寂静是什么？）→答（三时间不执取，名与身渴求消尽）→结果（不再进入老与死）。

The Jatukaṇṇī Sutta moves from a question about what freedom from desire looks like to a three-part instruction (suppress craving for pleasures; practise non-grasping across past, present, and future; let name-and-form craving exhaust itself) and a clear result (no re-entry into the aging-and-death domain). The three-time formula — past withered, future not grown, present not grasped — is one of the Pārāyanavagga's most memorable summaries of the temporal dimension of liberation: the sage does not merely let go of objects but lets go of all three temporal modes of grasping. 'Walking in the world in peace' confirms again that the Pārāyanavagga envisions liberation as a quality of presence in the world, not a withdrawal from it.
